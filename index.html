① Statistics
Understanding Data
Foundations

What is Data?

Data is information collected for analysis. Statistics helps us organise, display, and interpret this information.


Discrete data can only take specific values.

Continuous data can take any value within an interval.

Important Vocabulary

Population vs Sample

Population = the entire group being studied.

Sample = a smaller group selected from the population.


Good samples should be random and unbiased.

Charts & Graphs
Graph Types

Bar Charts vs Histograms

  • Bar charts use separated bars
  • Histograms are for continuous data
  • Histogram bars touch each other
  • Area represents frequency in histograms
Angle = (Frequency ÷ Total) × 360° Used to calculate pie chart sectors.
Data Interpretation

Scatterplots & Correlation

Scatterplots help identify relationships between variables.


  • Positive correlation
  • Negative correlation
  • No correlation
  • Strong or weak relationships
Dispersion

Box-and-Whisker Plots

These show the spread of data using:

  • Minimum
  • Lower quartile (Q1)
  • Median
  • Upper quartile (Q3)
  • Maximum
IQR = Q3 − Q1 Interquartile range measures the spread of the middle 50% of the data.
Measures of Central Tendency
Key Calculations

Mean, Median & Mode

  • Mean: arithmetic average
  • Median: middle value
  • Mode: most frequent value
② Probability
Probability Basics
0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1 Probability values range from impossible (0) to certain (1).
Foundations

Relative Frequency

Relative frequency estimates probability experimentally.

P(A) = Successful Outcomes ÷ Total Outcomes
Combined Events
Addition Rule

Adding Probabilities

Used when finding the probability of A or B occurring.

P(A ∪ B)=P(A)+P(B)−P(A ∩ B)
Multiplication Rule

Independent Events

Multiply probabilities for independent events happening together.

P(A ∩ B)=P(A) × P(B)
Advanced

Conditional Probability

Conditional probability changes the sample space using given information.

P(A|B)=P(A ∩ B)/P(B)
Tree Diagrams

With & Without Replacement

Tree diagrams organise probabilities visually.

  • Multiply along branches
  • Add probabilities at the ends
  • Without replacement changes probabilities
Worked Examples
Finding the Mean FOUNDATION
Find the mean of: 4, 7, 9, 10, 10
1
Add all values.
4 + 7 + 9 + 10 + 10 = 40
2
Divide by the number of values.
40 ÷ 5 = 8
Mean = 8
Probability of a Dice Roll BASICS
Find the probability of rolling an even number on a fair die.
1
Identify even outcomes.
2, 4, 6
2
Count successful outcomes.
3 successful outcomes
3
Divide by total outcomes.
3/6 = 1/2
Probability = 1/2
Conditional Probability ADVANCED
A bag contains 5 red and 3 blue counters. One red counter is removed. Find the probability the next counter is blue.
1
After removing one red, there are 7 counters left.
2
Blue counters remain unchanged.
3 blue counters
3
Calculate probability.
3/7
Probability = 3/7
Quick Cheat Sheet

Statistics

  • Mean = total ÷ number
  • Median = middle value
  • Mode = most frequent
  • Range = max − min

Graphs

  • Scatterplots show relationships
  • Box plots show spread
  • Cumulative frequency increases

Probability

  • 0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1
  • Multiply along branches
  • Add separate outcomes
  • Without replacement changes totals

Correlation

  • Negative correlation

Practice Questions
1. Find the median of: 3, 6, 8, 11, 15
Median = 8
2. Find the range of: 5, 9, 12, 18
Range = 18 − 5 = 13
3. A coin is tossed twice. Find the probability of getting two heads.
1/4
4. Describe the correlation shown in a scatterplot where points rise from left to right.
Positive correlation
5. A bag has 4 red and 6 blue counters. Find the probability of selecting a blue counter.
6/10 = 3/5
Common Mistakes
Exam Traps

Things students often lose marks for

  • Leaving gaps in histograms
  • Confusing median and mean
  • Forgetting to simplify probabilities
  • Adding probabilities instead of multiplying
  • Misreading cumulative frequency graphs
  • Assuming correlation means causation
